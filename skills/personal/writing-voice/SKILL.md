# Writing Voice

Pedro's communication style is conversational and direct. When refining his messages, preserve that voice — don't elevate it into something he wouldn't actually say.

## Core Principles

- **Conversational, not corporate.** Write like a competent person talking to another competent person.
- **Direct but not cold.** Gets to the point without feeling abrupt or unfriendly.
- **No over-apologising.** Cut "Sorry to bother you", "I hope this finds you well", "Please don't hesitate to reach out."
- **Short sentences.** If it can be two sentences, make it two.
- **No filler.** Cut throat-clearing phrases, unnecessary context, and jargon: "synergy", "circle back", "moving forward", "as per my last email". Cut excessive hedging: "I was just wondering if maybe…"

## PR Review Replies

When responding to a reviewer's suggestion Pedro disagrees with, the tone is explanatory not assertive.

- Don't declare the reviewer wrong — frame it as "here's what I was thinking."
- Use hedged language: "somewhat", "i'd leave it here", "i think", "it felt cleaner to me."
- Lead with the practical consequence, end with a soft conclusion.
- No "actually" or "the reason is" — just state it naturally.

> "If i move there it will be somewhat hidden. By having it in here we can understand what is happening. Because of this XML <> Compose interoperability i'd leave it here."

## Leadership messages

Team updates, recognition, and bad news have their own shapes — which framework to reach for,
what each message type looks like on the page. That lives in the `leadership-messages` skill,
and it takes precedence on structure. This skill still governs the voice underneath.

The two overlap on purpose, since each has to stand on its own. Where only one of them speaks
to a rule, that one applies: em dashes, for instance, are out in leadership messages and
unrestricted everywhere else.

## Format

- Match the channel — Slack is shorter than email.
- Bullet points are fine — but don't force structure where prose flows better.
- If offering multiple options, keep them short and clearly differentiated.
